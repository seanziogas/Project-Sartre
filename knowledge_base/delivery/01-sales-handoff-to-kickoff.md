# Sales Handoff to Client Kickoff

## Overview

This document covers the full process from the moment a deal closes to the end of the client kickoff call — and the activation phase that follows. This is the most critical transition in any engagement. The quality of the handoff and kickoff sets the tone for everything that comes after.

**Process sequence:**

1. Deal closes → Sales fills out the Handoff Document
2. Internal Handoff Call — Sales walks the pod through the handoff
3. Pre-Kickoff Preparation — GTME reviews, prepares, identifies gaps
4. Client Kickoff Call — Pod meets the client, aligns on structure and roadmap
5. Post-Kickoff Follow-Up — Recap, action items, operational setup
6. Activation Phase — First 1–2 weeks post-kickoff; access, exploration, scoping

---

## Step 1: The Sales Handoff Document

### What It Is

The Sales Handoff Document is a structured brief that sales completes when a deal closes. It gives the pod everything they need to walk into the internal handoff call fully informed — and to eventually run the client kickoff without needing to ask basic questions the sales team already answered.

The document is not optional and is not the GTME's job to fill out. Sales owns it. The GTME's job is to read it critically, identify gaps, and prepare questions for the internal handoff call.

### Sections

**Section 1: Project Overview**

Basic engagement logistics. Includes:
- Client name, website, LinkedIn
- Annual revenue
- Employee count
- Time zone
- Engagement type (Fixed Term, Month-to-Month, etc.)
- Engagement start and end dates
- Monthly rate

**Section 2: Stakeholders & Roles**

Two tables: one for the client team, one for The Kiln pod.

For each client stakeholder, the document should capture:
- Name and title
- Their role in the engagement (economic buyer, champion, delivery contact, executive sponsor, etc.)
- Notes on what they care about, their communication style, and any context needed to manage the relationship well

This section is critical for the GTME. Stakeholder notes determine how to open the kickoff call, who to prioritize building rapport with, and which relationships carry the most commercial weight.

**Section 3: Sales Conversation Recap**

This is the meat of the document. Includes:

- *Brief Company Summary* — what the company does, how they make money, their industry, funding stage, buyer personas, segments they sell into
- *CRM Health Score* — a 1–10 assessment of the client's CRM data quality
- *AI Literacy + Tech Savviness* — a 1–10 assessment of how technically sophisticated the client team is
- *Summary of Discussion* — a concise narrative of what was discussed across sales calls, the core problem, and the themes that emerged
- *Key Decisions & Expectations Set* — any commitments made during the sales process: timelines, modular track structure, pricing terms, pace of delivery expectations, anything the client might hold the engagement to
- *Pain Points & Success Criteria* — the client's stated problems and what "done" looks like for them, quantified wherever possible
- *Primary KPIs* — the metrics the engagement will be measured against
- *Risks / Watchouts* — anything that could complicate delivery: difficult stakeholders, internal blockers, data access limitations, sensitive topics, compliance constraints, or unrealistic expectations that weren't fully corrected in the sales process

**Section 4: GTM Stack**

A table of the client's existing toolstack across: data orchestration (Clay), CRM, sequencer, inbound lead routing, call recording, data warehouse, integration platforms, marketing automation, ABM, signals, data providers, and other. Includes Clay plan tier and notes on how each tool is currently used.

**Section 5: Checklist**

Operational setup items that must be completed before or at kickoff:
- Internal Slack channel created
- External Slack channel created (the shared channel with the client)
- SOW linked
- Fathom call recordings linked
- Monday board created
- Kickoff call scheduled (date, time, calendar link)
- Renewal check-in date set (~1 month before engagement end)
- Clay workbooks set up in client's Clay instance

Plus a *Client Materials* section for any assets the client has shared (account lists, content categories, lead scoring logic, territory assignments, etc.) and a *Fathom Calls* section linking to all recorded sales conversations.

### Rillet Example

The Rillet handoff document is an example of a well-executed handoff. It includes detailed stakeholder notes with explicit demeanor guidance for each contact, a thorough sales recap with specific quotes from the buyer, a full risk/watchout section, and a client materials checklist that flags exactly what the pod still needs from the client before specific tracks can begin. Use it as a benchmark for the level of detail expected.

## Step 2: Internal Handoff Call

### What It Is

A call between sales and the full pod (MD + GTME + TOS) to walk through the handoff document before the client kickoff. This is not optional. The handoff document alone is not sufficient — the nuance, tone, and relationship context that live in the sales person's head need to be transferred verbally.

### Who Attends

- The salesperson who ran the deal (required)
- MD (required)
- GTME (required)
- TOS (required — they need the client context to build well)

### What to Cover

The GTME should come to this call with the handoff doc already read and a prepared list of questions. The call isn't a read-aloud of the document — it's a dialogue to fill the gaps.

Key topics:
- **Stakeholder dynamics**: Who is the real decision maker? Who can slow things down? What's the relationship between the economic buyer and the day-to-day contact? Any interpersonal dynamics the pod should know about?
- **Expectations that may not be in writing**: Did the client say anything off-the-record about pace, competitors, internal politics, or what failure looks like for them?
- **Risks the doc doesn't fully capture**: What's the sales person most worried about? What's the one thing that could blow up this engagement?
- **What got them to "yes"**: What was the specific thing that closed the deal? Knowing what landed can help the GTME reinforce that value early.
- **Open items before kickoff**: What does the client still need to provide? What has the pod not yet received?
- **Tone guidance for the kickoff**: How should the GTME open? Is the client formal or casual? Do they want to move fast, or are they more methodical?

### GTME's Job After the Call

By the end of the internal handoff call, the GTME should be able to run the client kickoff independently. They should know every stakeholder, understand the commercial context, have a clear view of the risks, and know exactly what they need to accomplish in the first 30 days to make the engagement feel like a success.

## Step 3: Pre-Kickoff Preparation

Between the internal handoff call and the client kickoff, the GTME prepares. The MD may assist depending on engagement complexity and GTME tenure.

**What the MD does:**

- Prepare the kickoff deck. The MD takes the standard template and customizes it with the client's name, engagement type, and track structure from the SOW. The deck should not be generic — it should feel specific to this client. Keep it tight; every slide that isn't necessary should be cut.
- Review the handoff doc and Fathom recordings to get sharp on this client's context, communication style, and risks before the call.

**What the GTME does:**

- Re-read the SOW in full. Know every track, every deliverable, every constraint.
- Watch the Fathom recordings if anything in the handoff doc is unclear or if the GTME needs to calibrate their sense of each stakeholder's personality.
- Prepare the questions they want to ask during the activation dialogue portion of the kickoff. What do they need to know to start Week 1? What access is outstanding? What are the biggest unknowns?
- Identify what client materials are still outstanding. Anything not yet received should be flagged as an action item for the kickoff call.
- Confirm logistics: who is attending the kickoff from the client side, what time zone they're in, who is optional vs. required.
- Set up the Monday board if not already done.
- Prepare the external Slack channel.

## Step 4: The Client Kickoff Call

### Purpose

The kickoff call accomplishes three things:
1. Establishes the working relationship and tone
2. Aligns the client on how the engagement will run (communication, structure, decision ownership)
3. Sets the roadmap for what gets built first and why

It is not a discovery call. Discovery happened in sales. The kickoff is forward-looking — it's about execution.

### Who Attends

**The Kiln side:**
- MD (required — attends all client calls during Month 1 of every new engagement)
- GTME (leads the call)
- TOS (optional — most kickoffs are GTME + MD only, TOS joins if client complexity warrants it)

**Client side:**
- Economic buyer / champion (required if possible)
- Day-to-day delivery contact (required)
- Any additional stakeholders whose alignment is needed for the engagement to move

For reference, the Rillet kickoff had Stephen (Head of Marketing / economic buyer) listed as optional due to calendar constraints, with Sarah (external advisor) and Jason (day-to-day) as confirmed. Even when the economic buyer can't attend, they should receive a follow-up summary the same day.

### Structure and Philosophy

The kickoff has two distinct parts: a short presentation run by the MD, followed by an activation dialogue co-led by the MD and GTME.

The presentation should be fast. Clients paying meaningful money don't want to spend their kickoff call on admin housekeeping — they want to feel like they're already moving. The goal is to get through the deck in as little time as possible, covering only what needs to be established up front, and then transition immediately into the activation phase questions. The faster you get into dialogue, the better the call feels.

The MD runs the deck. The MD also prepares it ahead of the call using the standard template, tailored with the client's name, engagement type, and track structure from the SOW.

### Agenda / Deck Structure

**Slide 1 — Title / Welcome**
Brief welcome and introductions. The MD opens the call, introduces The Kiln team, and invites the client to do the same. Keep this tight — the goal is familiarity, not extended bios.

**Slide 2 — Team Introductions**
Introduce The Kiln team (MD, GTME, TOS if present) and invite the client to introduce themselves. Use this to establish warmth before moving into structure.

**Slide 3 — Communication Norms**
Set expectations for how the engagement will run day-to-day:
- *Monday.com* — The Kiln's project tracking tool. The client can view progress at any time. The GTME keeps it updated.
- *Slack* — Primary async communication channel. A dedicated shared channel (e.g., #the-kiln) will be the hub. A separate channel will be created for each major track where high-volume activity happens (e.g., #linkedin-high-engagers for Track A). The GTME posts updates on a Monday/Wednesday/Friday cadence.
- *Weekly Call* — A 30–60 minute call each week. The standing time should be confirmed at kickoff.

**Slide 4 — Partnership Structure**
Clarify who owns what. This is important for managing scope expectations from day one.

*The Kiln owns:*
- Clay architecture and execution
- Data sourcing and enrichment logic
- System builds and integrations
- QA before anything goes live

*Client owns:*
- Goals and success criteria
- Stakeholder alignment internally
- Providing access and resources (CRM, tools, account lists, etc.)
- Feedback and iteration direction

This framing matters. It positions The Kiln as an execution partner, not a strategy consultant — and makes clear that blocked access or missing inputs are the client's responsibility to resolve.

**Slide 5 — Partnership Roadmap**
A high-level view of the engagement timeline split into two phases:
- *Activation Phase* — First 1–2 weeks. No building. Access, exploration, scoping.
- *Building Phase* — The bulk of the engagement. Executing the tracks in the SOW.

This slide helps the client understand why building doesn't start on Day 1 — the activation phase is necessary groundwork, not delay.

**Slide 6 — Activation Phase Roadmap**
What specifically happens in the activation phase:
- Tool access and permissions setup
- CRM exploration and data audit
- Project scoping call (locking the first track's scope before building)
- Slack channel setup and onboarding

Confirm any outstanding access requests here. If the client hasn't yet given Clay or CRM access, this is the moment to surface it and set a deadline.

**Slide 7 — Building Phase / Track Roadmap**
Walk through the tracks in the SOW. For each track:
- What it does (brief, client-language description)
- What they'll be able to do after it's live
- Any dependencies or client inputs required before it can begin

Confirm the starting track and the sequencing logic. For engagements with multiple tracks (like the Rillet LinkedIn bundle), confirm the client's understanding that Phase 1 is all three tracks running as a connected motion.

### Transition to Activation Dialogue

Once the deck is done, the MD transitions directly into the activation phase. Something like: "Okay, that's all the housekeeping — let's get into what we actually need to do to get moving." From here, the call becomes a working dialogue co-led by the MD and GTME. The GTME should be actively involved: asking questions, gathering context, identifying blockers to access, and starting to build a picture of what Week 1 looks like.

This is where the real kickoff happens. The deck is the preamble.

### What to Accomplish by the End of the Kickoff

Before leaving the kickoff call, confirm:
- The weekly call time is set
- All access requests are submitted or have a deadline
- The starting track is confirmed and scoped
- Any outstanding client materials have owners and timelines
- The GTME's Slack handle is shared and they are (or will be) in the client's Slack

## Step 5: Post-Kickoff Follow-Up

Immediately after the kickoff call, the GTME sends a follow-up covering:

- **Recap of the call** — key points discussed, decisions made, anything confirmed verbally that should be in writing
- **Action items for both sides** — The Kiln's items and the client's items, each with owners and timelines
- **Any follow-up questions** that came up during the call but weren't fully resolved

This follow-up goes to all kickoff attendees. If the economic buyer wasn't on the kickoff, they get a version too.

### Operational Setup After Kickoff

The following should be completed in the 24–48 hours after the kickoff call:

- **Monday board** — fully built out and shared with the client. If it was set up before kickoff, confirm the client has access and can navigate it.
- **Slack channels** — external shared channel created, GTME and TOS added to the client's Slack workspace, track-specific channels created if applicable.
- **Weekly recurring meeting** — booked and sent. The standing time was confirmed on the kickoff call; this makes it calendar reality.
- **Calls with CRM/Salesforce admin and AE/BDR** — get these on the calendar during the first week. These are not optional: direct access to the people who run the CRM and who will use the outputs is essential before building starts. Scheduling them early prevents them from becoming blockers later.
- **Any other ad hoc action items** from the kickoff call — if something came up that doesn't fit cleanly into the above, it still needs an owner and a deadline tracked in Monday.

## Step 6: Activation Phase

The activation phase is the first 1–2 weeks of the engagement. No new system builds happen during this phase. The goal is to gather everything needed to build the right thing.

### What Happens

**Tool access and permissions**
The GTME needs access to every relevant system before building starts: Clay (client workspace), CRM (Salesforce, HubSpot, or Attio), sequencer, data warehouse if applicable, and any tools specific to the engagement. The GTME tracks outstanding access items in Monday.com and follows up proactively — do not wait for the client to push.

**CRM audit**
The GTME (and TOS, directed by the GTME) conducts an initial audit of the client's CRM. Key questions:
- What data exists and what's missing? (contact/company fields, enrichment coverage, location data)
- What's the record volume? (contact count, company count, active pipeline)
- What's the data quality? (duplicates, stale records, incomplete ownership)
- How does data flow between tools? (e.g., HubSpot ↔ Salesforce bi-directional sync, what breaks)
- What field structure exists and what does the pod need to understand before building routing or enrichment logic?

The CRM audit doesn't need to be exhaustive in Week 1 — it needs to be sufficient to make good build decisions for Track 1.

**Stakeholder introductions**
The GTME should aim to meet as many people on the client team as possible during the activation phase. Day-to-day delivery contact, BDR team lead, relevant ops or RevOps contacts, and anyone who will be a downstream user of what gets built. These conversations reveal context that doesn't exist in the handoff doc: real workflow, informal workarounds, who actually reviews what, where the friction is.

For the Rillet engagement: Jason Weed was the day-to-day delivery contact and had hands-on Clay capacity. Meeting him early and involving him in the routing logic build — rather than handing him a finished system — was explicitly identified as the right approach.

**Project scoping session**
Before the first track enters build phase, the GTME runs a focused scoping session (async or a short call) to confirm:
- The exact scope of Track 1 (what's in, what's out)
- Any client inputs still needed before building can start
- Acceptance criteria — what does "done" look like for this track?

This prevents scope creep and misaligned expectations later. Get written confirmation (Slack message or Monday update) for anything that expands timelines.

**Slack and Monday.com setup**
- GTME and TOS are added to client Slack
- External shared channel is set up
- Track-specific channels are created if needed
- Monday board is active and shared with the client
- Client is briefed on how to use Monday.com if they're new to it

### Activation Phase Success Criteria

By the end of the activation phase:
- The pod has access to all tools needed for Track 1
- CRM data is understood well enough to make design decisions
- Key client stakeholders have been met and are engaged
- Track 1 scope is locked and confirmed in writing
- The working relationship is established — the GTME has a direct relationship with the day-to-day contact that doesn't run through the economic buyer

---

## Key Principles for This Phase

**Month 1 is the engagement.** If the first month doesn't surface real, qualified value, the engagement will feel like overhead regardless of what happens in Months 2–4. Everything in the handoff, kickoff, and activation phase is in service of delivering something tangible before the first month ends.

**Always be shipping.** Don't wait for a weekly call to share progress. Share work as it's built, not at the end of a sprint. Most clients expect fast motion.

**The GTME is the relationship.** The MD attends all calls in Month 1, but the GTME leads. The goal is for the client to feel that the GTME is their primary point of contact. By the end of the activation phase, the GTME should have a direct relationship with the day-to-day contact that doesn't run through the MD.

**Modular flexibility requires scope clarity.** Every track substitution or sequence change requires explicit written confirmation before building begins. The flexibility is a feature, not a license to build anything without alignment.
