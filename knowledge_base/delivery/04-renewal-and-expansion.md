# Renewal and Expansion

## Overview

The Kiln's engagement model is built for extension. Most contracts are 3–6 months, with renewals and expansions being the primary growth driver. A well-run engagement doesn't end — it evolves.

Renewal and expansion conversations are owned jointly by the **MD and the Sales Team**. The GTME's job is to deliver work that makes renewal obvious. The MD and sales work together to frame it commercially and close it.

---

## Key Dates

Every engagement should have two critical dates tracked from Day 1:

1. **Engagement end date** — the last day of the current contract term
2. **Renewal check-in date** — approximately 45 days before the engagement end date

The renewal check-in date is set at kickoff and tracked in Monday.com. The MD owns flagging it. Missing this window is not acceptable — renewal conversations that start in the final week rarely close cleanly.

For shorter engagements (3 months or less), 30 days out may be the practical starting point, but 45 days is always preferred when feasible. The target window is 45–30 days out — earlier is better, and it's okay if the conversation starts closer to 30 days when circumstances require it.

---

## Types of Outcomes

### Extension
The client continues the engagement. Extensions can take a few different forms, in order of preference:

1. **6-month contract** — the best outcome. Locking in another 6 months of work provides stability for both sides and signals strong client confidence.
2. **Month-to-month with 60-day cancellation policy** — the second-best option. Provides flexibility for the client while giving The Kiln meaningful runway.
3. **3-month contract** — acceptable, but not ideal. Use this as a fallback when the client isn't ready to commit longer-term.

The MD and sales team should always be pushing for a 6-month extension first. Walk down to the other options only if needed.

### Expansion
Rarer than an extension, and a clear signal that the engagement has gone exceptionally well. An expansion means the client both extends and increases capacity — they want more. The only way to offer expansion is in the same building blocks as the original contract: the client doubles or triples their engagement. We can't offer fractional capacity increases (e.g., 1.5x) as these are operationally disruptive. In practice, most expansions are a doubling of capacity.

### Churn
An engagement that doesn't renew. There are two versions:

- **Good churn** — the client genuinely ran out of greenfield work and there's nothing meaningful left to build. The relationship is positive; they may come back when new needs emerge, and they're likely to be a reference or case study.
- **Bad churn** — the client has more work to do but is taking it in-house or to another agency. This reflects either poor delivery quality or a poor client experience. It requires an honest MD-level post-mortem.

## The Renewal Process

### ~45 Days Out — MD Takes Stock
The MD syncs with the GTME to assess renewal posture. This doesn't need to be a long conversation — the goal is to get aligned quickly and build a game plan:
- What was delivered vs. what was scoped?
- What outstanding work remains?
- What's the client's sentiment? Satisfied, neutral, or at risk?
- What additional use cases or problems have surfaced during the engagement?
- What's the renewal pitch — what would we do in the next term?

The MD and sales team use this input to shape the approach before going back to the client.

### ~40 Days Out — Initiate Client Conversation
The MD gets time on the calendar with the client — either on an existing weekly call or a dedicated conversation. The goal at this stage is to open the "what's next" dialogue naturally, not to deliver a formal pitch. Frame what's been accomplished, acknowledge what has momentum, and surface what The Kiln could tackle next.

This conversation should never feel like a cold ask. If the engagement has gone well and the GTME has built a strong relationship, the renewal should feel like the obvious next move for the client.

### Proposal (if needed)
For straightforward extensions (same scope, same rate), a simple email confirmation or updated SOW is sufficient. For expansions or scope changes, a new proposal may be required. The MD drafts this in collaboration with sales, incorporating what the GTME learned during the engagement.

## Building Toward Renewal During Delivery

The best renewals are won in Month 1, not Month 5. The GTME's job throughout the engagement is to:

1. **Surface new problems as they're discovered.** When the GTME spots a use case or gap that wasn't in the original SOW, they flag it to the MD — not as a scope addition right now, but as a future opportunity. The MD logs these.

2. **Maintain the impact narrative.** The GTME tracks what was delivered and what it did — in business terms, not technical terms. "We built a deduplication workflow" is not the impact narrative. "We reduced manual CRM work for the SDR team by an estimated 3 hours/week and improved domain coverage from 60% to 91%" is.

3. **Keep client sentiment strong.** Renewals don't happen when clients feel like they haven't gotten value. The GTME is responsible for making sure the client feels the work is moving and that The Kiln is an indispensable part of their GTM stack.

4. **Don't hide problems.** Unresolved issues that surface at renewal time torpedo deals. Surface friction early, resolve it, and document that it was handled. A client who saw a problem get solved is more likely to renew than one who never saw a problem — but only if the GTME handled it with transparency and competence.

## Expansion Signals to Watch For

The GTME should flag these to the MD immediately when they surface during an engagement:

- Client mentions a problem outside the current track scope
- Client asks "could you also do X?" during a weekly call
- Client shares a pain point in another department (e.g., current engagement is with Sales, but Marketing ops is also broken)
- Client's team grows — more reps or markets create new automation needs
- Engagement delivers strong results — client wants to replicate the motion in a new territory or segment

---

## Clean Engagement Close-Out

When an engagement ends (renewal or not), the GTME is responsible for a clean handoff:

- All Monday.com tasks are updated to final status
- All Clay workbooks are documented and left in a maintainable state
- Any credentials, API keys, or access that The Kiln controlled are noted for the client
- A brief engagement summary is written (what was built, what it does, how to maintain it) and shared with the client
- Fathom recordings and SOW are linked in the internal Slack channel for future reference

Even if the client doesn't renew, the handoff should be clean enough that they'd come back.
