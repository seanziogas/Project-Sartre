# Deal Stages Guide — The Kiln

A rep-ready reference for every stage of a Kiln deal. Each section opens with exit criteria — the minimum bar to advance. Everything below it is how you get there.

---

## Two Frameworks Running in Parallel

Think of these as **rocket + fuel**. The rocket gets you from stage to stage. The fuel is what propels it forward. Both are required — process without skill is academically strong, commercially weak. Skill without process is chaos.

**MEDDPICC is the rocket — the process.** It tells you *what* information to gather and when. It's a deal qualification framework, not a sales methodology — it tells you whether a deal is healthy, not how to run a conversation. Work it in this order:
- **WHY first:** (I) Identified Pain → (M) Metrics
- **WHO next:** (C) Champion → (E) Economic Buyer — earned after pain is established
- **HOW:** (Dc) Decision Criteria → (Dp) Decision Process → (P) Paper — criteria before SOW, process/paper later
- **WHO ELSE:** (Co) Competitors → Partners when applicable

**The Narrative Arc is the fuel — the skill.** It tells you *how* to run the conversations that move a deal forward. Heavy in Discovery and Scoping, carried through to close:

| # | Element | Stage Where It's Built | Purpose |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | **Market Forces** — why now | Discovery | Create urgency by connecting their pain to external pressure |
| 2 | **Current Challenges** — their pain | Discovery | Establish the problem in their words, with a number attached |
| 3 | **Future Vision** — the dream | Discovery → Scoping | Get them to articulate what good looks like — then hold them to it |
| 4 | **Your Solution** — the bridge | Scoping (SOW) | Show how we close the gap between current and future |
| 5 | **Proof Points** — why believe | Scoping → SOW | References, case studies, real workflows matched to their situation |
| 6 | **Next Steps** — the path | Every stage | Always leave with a defined action and a date |

Every great discovery call hits all six. The SOW crystallizes all six in writing. The EB presentation walks an executive through all six in person.

## Stage 1: Pre-Qual — 15%

### Exit Criteria
- [ ] Company is a plausible ICP fit (B2B SaaS or enterprise, 50+ employees, GTM team exists)
- [ ] A real problem has been stated or inferred — not just "we heard about Clay" **(I: surface-level pain confirmed)**
- [ ] Budget timing is in the current or next fiscal year
- [ ] A named internal contact is willing to take a first call
- [ ] No hard disqualifiers present (see below)

### Our Questions at This Stage
- "What challenges were you hoping to solve by taking this call?"
- "Whats driving the urgency to fix this? Who else at the org feels it? What have you done to try and solve it? What happens if you dont"
    - insert names when needed?
- "When do you need these issues solved by?" Why and what happens if they dont get solved?"
- "Whats the gap between where they are at grom a gtm ai autoamtion standpoint vs. where they want to be?"
- "There are lots of things to tackle from a GTM perspective - crm enrichment, scoring priotization, signals, inbound, outbound - do you have a plan on wihch things need to get addressED first and can you quantify why it has a high priority?"
- "have you decided on needing an agency to help solve this? Why not internal? If agency, what matters to you when deciding which one?"
- "Who else would be involved in a decision like this?"
- "What does your current outbound/enrichment motion look like today?"
- If an sdr were to walk in today, do they have a list of enriched accounts, priortized by fit/signals, contacts enriched, company researched, and messaging partially crafted?" Whats the gap?
- "What's your timeline for making a change?"

### Hard Disqualifiers (Stop Here)
- THey only need help with 1-2 things, and dont want a longer 3 month engagemnt.
- Very small GTM Team (usually Series A and under cant justify price)
- No budget until next fiscal year and no urgency driver
- No internal champion — dependent on a single evaluator who can't decide
- Expects a 1-month engagement — expectation mismatch will surface at scoping

### Common Blockers
- Inbound came in from someone who's exploring but has no mandate
- Company is mid-tool selection — looking for a vendor comparison, not an agency
- Decision-maker is unreachable or hasn't been identified

## Stage 2: Discovery — 25%

The intro call. Before any pain questions, any metrics, any talk of what we build — your job is to construct a shared reality.

*Here's where the world is going. Here's why it has to happen this way. Here's where you need to be in five years — and here's how we get there together. Once there's alignment on the future, the product sells itself.*

The AI-native companies already believe this — they came to you. The prospect who needs the most from you is the one who hasn't fully bought in yet: the 2X-referred deal, the late adopter, the team where pipeline looks fine and the AI mandate feels abstract. Don't rush to pain. Build the vision first. When the destination is agreed on, the pain becomes obvious and the solution becomes inevitable.

MEDDPICC focus: **(I) Pain → (M) Metrics → (C) Champion**

Narrative arc focus: **Market Forces → Current Challenges → Future Vision → (begin) Proof Points → Next Steps**

### Exit Criteria
- [ ] **Market forces** — prospect has connected their pain to external pressure (AI disruption, headcount scrutiny, board pipeline pressure, competitive GTM arms race)
- [ ] **(I) Pain** — buyer has shared at least 4 critical operational or technical gaps, articulated in their own words, not inferred by the rep
- [ ] Pain confirmed across more than one person in the org where possible
- [ ] **Current state / future state / impact** — buyer has described where they are today, where they want to be, and quantified the business impact of the gap (time, pipeline, headcount, cost, or revenue)
- [ ] **(M) Metrics** — at least one quantified baseline number and one target number attached to the pain
- [ ] What they've tried before and why it didn't work
- [ ] What happens if this doesn't get solved (consequence and urgency)
- [ ] How they'd justify the spend internally (their internal ROI narrative)
- [ ] Named urgency driver — event, hire, initiative, exec pressure, deadline
- [ ] **Future vision** captured — they've described what "great" looks like in their words
- [ ] **(C) Champion** identified — someone internal who wants this solved and has credibility
- [ ] Economic Buyer identified by name or title (even if not in the room yet)
- [ ] Mutual commitment to a scoping call with defined attendees

### 1. Market Forces — Why Now

Before diving into their pain, establish that the problem is bigger than their company. The goal isn't to scare them — it's to reframe timing. Most prospects think they have the luxury of getting to this "when things settle down." They don't.

**The framing** *(further reading: [The Shackleton Moment — How AI Is Creating a Permanent Divide in Sales](https://www.linkedin.com/pulse/shackleton-moment-how-ai-creating-permanent-divide-sales-khanna-ax9de/))*:

Ramp tracked revenue growth across 50,000+ businesses from 2023 to 2025. Companies in the top 25% of AI spend grew revenue approximately 100%. Companies spending nothing on AI grew roughly 20% — in line with the broader economy. Same market, same window, 5x difference. The gap isn't a prediction. It's already in the data, and it's compounding. *(Source: Ramp Economics Lab, ramp.com/data)*

Revenue is a lagging indicator of the systems underneath it. If it's steady, that's not necessarily reassuring — it may mean the drop, when it comes, will be more drastic and sudden. While your number holds, other teams are compounding on systems investments. The gap builds invisibly. By the time the number moves, it's already too late to close it quickly.

The teams pulling away right now aren't necessarily bigger or better funded. They're compressing the distance between signal and action — redesigning how they identify demand, route leads, prep reps, detect expansion, and drive renewals. Not just automating manual tasks, but increasing the speed and quality of every decision their revenue team makes.

On the other side: teams still running GTM as a series of handoffs, dashboards, and human follow-up. Pipeline looks fine. Headcount is growing. That stability is the trap.

The same dynamic playing out in engineering — where the engineers who understand what to build are pulling away from the ones who only knew how to build — is now happening in sales. The reps and teams with AI-powered infrastructure behind them aren't just faster. They're making better calls, reaching the right people first, and compounding that advantage every quarter.

**Questions to surface market forces:**
- "Has anything changed in the last 12-18 months that's making this more urgent — new exec, board pressure, competitive movement?"
- "When you look at your best competitors, do you have a sense of how they're running their GTM motion differently?"
- "Is leadership measuring pipeline efficiency differently than they were two years ago — fewer resources, higher targets?"
- "What does your pipeline look like right now — and does that number feel like an honest signal or a lagging one?"
- "What happens to the gap between you and the teams moving fastest if you don't solve this in the next year?"

**The goal:** They should recognize themselves on one side of that line — and feel the pull toward the other. The cost of waiting isn't zero; it's compounding.

### 2. Current Challenges — Their Pain

Surface the pain specifically, with a number. Don't accept vague answers. Run this across multiple people in the room — different roles feel the pain differently, and surfacing that creates internal urgency.

**The 5-Question Pain Framework (run in order):**

1. **What's the pain?** — Get them to name the specific problem in their own words. Don't lead.
2. **What are the metrics behind the pain?** — Quantify it. Hours, pipeline, headcount, cost. If they can't name a number, help them estimate.
3. **What have they already tried to fix it?** — Establishes credibility, rules out easy alternatives, shows whether they're serious.
4. **What happens if this doesn't get solved?** — Consequence drives urgency. Make them say it out loud.
5. **How would they justify the spend to fix this?** — The internal ROI narrative they'll carry to the EB. If they can't answer this, you have work to do.

**Questions to go deeper:**
- "Walk me through your current process step by step — where does it start, where does it break?"
- "How are signals getting to your reps today — or are they?"
- "Why hasn't this been solved already?"
- "Who else in the org feels this pain — is this just a marketing problem or does sales feel it too?"

**Metrics — Don't Accept Vague Answers**

"We want an AI-powered GTM engine" is not a metric. Push until you have a number. The test: *Can we use this number to prove the engagement worked or didn't?*

- "How would you measure whether a good GTM engine is better than a bad one? What's the actual number?"
- "Is it pipeline dollars per SDR? Qualified calls per day? Conversion rate from outreach to meeting?"
- "What does a good week look like for an SDR right now — and what would a great week look like after this?"
- "How many Revenue Generating Activities are your AEs doing per day? What should that number be?"
- "How many qualified opportunities is each SDR or AE generating right now — and what's the target?"
- "What's your closed/won revenue per AE today? What would it need to be to make this worth it?"
- "How much time per week are reps or ops spending on this manually — and what would zero look like?"

**Leave with:** at least one baseline number and one target number. That gap is the business case.

**Pain points that hit hardest (from 24+ transcripts):**
1. Manual research burning BDR/rep time — "BDRs doing 50 tasks a day just to send one email"
2. Signals not mapped or not actionable — they don't know what to track, or it's tracked and sitting unused
3. Tools not talking to each other — Clay, HubSpot, SFDC, ZoomInfo all siloed
4. CRM data quality broken — "We have 240K accounts but only 20K are usable"
5. Can't scale without proportional headcount — "We'd need to hire 3x to do this manually"
6. Previous build failed — agency tables that died, internal RevOps solutions that didn't last
7. Speed-to-lead and routing failures — leads hitting black holes, manual triage eating exec time
8. Tool paralysis — "Should we use Clay or Claude or n8n?" That confusion is the problem

### 3. Future Vision — The Dream

Once pain is established, flip it. Get them to articulate what good looks like — in their words, not yours. This becomes the north star for the SOW and the EB presentation.

**Questions to surface the future vision:**
- "If we solved this perfectly, what does your team's day look like 6 months from now?"
- "What does a great week look like for your SDRs or AEs when this is working?"
- "If you could point your EB to one number that proves this worked, what would it be?"
- "What does your GTM motion look like when it's running the way you've always wanted it to?"
- "In an ideal world, what are reps spending their time on — and what's the machine handling?"

**Why this matters:** The future vision is what you'll sell back to them in the SOW and the EB meeting. Write it down verbatim. "You told me the dream was [X] — here's how we get there" is more powerful than any pitch deck.

---

### 4. Proof Points — Why Believe (Begin Here)

Don't wait until scoping to establish credibility. Drop one relevant proof point in discovery — enough to show you've done this before, not enough to turn the call into a pitch.

**Trust-building moves that work:**
- **Turn-down stories** — "We told [client] they weren't ready and pointed them somewhere else. Here's what that looked like." Builds trust fast.
- **Validate cheaper alternatives** — If a simpler tool might work, say so. Honesty builds more trust than it loses.
- **Warm referrals** — "You should talk to [reference in their space]." Drop before the call ends.
- **Show a real workflow** — Not slides. A quick screen share of an actual Clay table beats any deck.

### 5. Next Steps — The Path

- Schedule the scoping call before you hang up. Define who needs to be there.
- Send a recap email within 24 hours:
  - **(I) Identified Pain** — in their language, not yours
  - **Players involved** — Champion, Decision Process contacts, Economic Buyer if identified
  - **(M) Desired Business Outcome** — the metric or result they're after
  - **Next step** — what's happening next and when

### Common Prospect Questions (and How We Answer)

| They Ask | How to Answer |
|---|---|
| "What is Clay / what does a claygency do?" | Clay is a data orchestration platform. We build the workflows inside it — sourcing, enriching, signaling, routing — so your team doesn't have to. We're not reselling Clay; we're the implementation layer. |
| "How are you different from ZoomInfo or Apollo?" | ZoomInfo is a data vendor. We're a systems integrator. We pull from Clay, ZoomInfo, LinkedIn, and 50+ other sources and build the logic that turns that data into action. |
| "How long does a project take?" | Minimum 3 months. Typically 4. Anything less and we can't build something that lasts — most agencies that promise fast hand you something that breaks when they leave. |
| "Who maintains this after you build it?" | We build with handoff in mind. By month 3, we're documenting, training, and ideally helping you hire a GTM engineer internally. We're not trying to create dependency. |
| "Are you biased toward Clay?" | We're Clay-native, yes. But we've pushed clients toward n8n, Claude, or doing nothing when Clay wasn't the right fit. If it's not right for you, we'll say so. |

### Common Blockers
- Prospect came in with no agenda and no real need — exploring, not buying
- No urgency driver — interesting problem, but "we'll get to it eventually" (market forces framing fixes this)
- Decision-maker not in the room and hard to access
- Expectation that this is a 1-month project — address before scoping

## Stage 3: Scoping — 50%

Pain is validated, vision is captured. Now you deepen the narrative — confirm the champion, map the EB path, understand decision criteria, and build the SOW as the full story: where they are, where they want to go, how we get there, why we're the right team to do it, and what it costs.

MEDDPICC focus: **(C) Champion → (E) Economic Buyer → (Dc) Decision Criteria → (Co) Competitors**

Narrative arc focus: **Reinforce Market Forces → Deepen Current Challenges → Lock in Future Vision → Your Solution (SOW) → Proof Points → Next Steps**

### Exit Criteria
- [ ] **(C) Champion** confirmed against all three criteria: (1) has personal pain tied to this problem, (2) has access to the economic buyer, (3) will fight for The Kiln when we're not in the room
- [ ] **(E) Economic Buyer** identified by name — EB must be on or scheduled for the scoping call before the SOW is written
- [ ] **(Dc) Decision Criteria** understood — speed, methodology, references, outcome certainty, pricing
- [ ] **(Co)** Competition identified — who else are they evaluating and who inside the org is advocating for them
- [ ] **Future vision** locked in and reflected in the SOW language
- [ ] SOW drafted — includes narrative arc, tracks, success criteria, business case, and pricing
- [ ] 30-day win defined — something concrete the client can show internally
- [ ] Budget confirmed for a 3-month minimum engagement
- [ ] Team bandwidth confirmed — client can respond daily
- [ ] Foundational readiness assessed — CRM hygiene, tool stack, data quality

### Gate: Before the SOW Is Written

Do not draft the SOW until all of the following are true. These must be completed during Scoping — they cannot be resolved after the fact.

**EB alignment:**
- [ ] Business problem, priority level, and desired outcomes confirmed by the economic buyer in their own words
- [ ] Critical operational gaps confirmed by EB and the broader group
- [ ] Economic buyer agrees The Kiln is a viable solution to evaluate
- [ ] Steps required to make a confident evaluation decision identified and agreed upon
- [ ] EB aligned on budget and timeline

**Buying committee:**
- [ ] Pain, authority, preference, and role of each buying committee member analyzed
- [ ] Potential champions confirmed against all three criteria: personal pain, EB access, will advocate when not in the room

If the EB hasn't confirmed these, do not write the SOW. Schedule the EB alignment call first.

### The SOW Is the Business Case

The SOW is where all six narrative arc elements come together in writing. There is no separate cost justification doc. Structure it as:

- **Market Forces** — why the timing matters (one paragraph; ties their pain to external pressure)
- **Current Challenges** — as-is state with metrics (the baseline number from discovery)
- **Future Vision** — to-be state in their words (the target number from discovery)
- **Your Solution** — tracks, projects, and workstreams that close the gap
- **Business Case** — the financial model (see below)
- **Proof Points** — relevant client reference or outcome matched to their situation
- **Success Criteria** — per track, tied directly to the business case numbers
- **Pricing** — presented as the investment in the ROI calculation, not a line item
- **Next Steps** — timeline, start date, kickoff structure

The champion should be able to hand this SOW to the EB and use it to get the deal approved without you in the room.

---

### The Business Case Section

This is what separates a proposal from a business case. The goal is for the EB to read this and see the engagement as a return, not an expense.

**The north star metric: Cost per Dollar Booked.**

Every initiative in the business case should show how it moves this number. Saving reps time is nice, but time savings are unrealized gains — they only get realized when they translate into more output per rep or fewer reps for the same output. Cost savings are nice too, but nobody enjoys cost-controlling their way to EBITDA growth. Revenue acceleration is the language the board speaks. More pipeline, higher conversion rates, shorter sales cycles. That is what earns attention and budget.

Build the business case around that language. The cost reduction side establishes credibility. The growth side is what closes the deal.

**The model has two sides: cost reduction and growth.**

#### Side 1 — Cost Reduction (Efficiency)

| Line | How to Calculate |
|---|---|
| **Current state costs ($X)** | Hours/week on manual tasks × hourly loaded cost of the person doing them × 52. Include: BDR research time, ops manual enrichment, exec triage time (e.g., Gary's 5 hrs/week = $X/yr), tool overlap/redundancy. |
| **Future state savings ($Y)** | What those same tasks cost when automated — typically near zero marginal cost. The delta is the saving. |
| **Net benefit ($Y - $X)** | The annual value of reclaimed time and eliminated waste. |
| **Investment required ($Z)** | The Kiln engagement cost (e.g., $20K/mo × 3 months = $60K). |
| **ROI ratio** | ($Y - $X) / $Z. A 3:1 ratio or better is the target. |

The cost side is usually easy to build from the metrics you captured in discovery. If Gary spends 5 hours/week on manual lead triage at a loaded cost of $150/hr, that's $39K/year — before you count the leads that fall through.

#### Side 2 — Growth Lever (Pipeline)

Cost savings alone is a defensive business case. Add the growth lever and it becomes offensive.

The question to answer: *What does one additional pipeline dollar cost to generate today, and what would it cost with the system in place?*

Build this from discovery metrics:

| Line | How to Calculate |
|---|---|
| **Current pipeline per SDR** | Qualified opps × average deal size (or pipeline $) per SDR per quarter |
| **Target pipeline per SDR** | What the prospect said good looks like — the north star metric from discovery |
| **Delta** | The gap between current and target, per rep, per quarter |
| **At scale** | Delta × number of reps × 4 quarters = incremental annual pipeline |
| **At your close rate** | Incremental pipeline × close rate = incremental revenue |

Example: If an SDR generates $400K in pipeline per quarter today and the target is $600K, that's $200K/SDR/quarter. Across 5 SDRs, that's $4M in incremental annual pipeline — at a 25% close rate, $1M in incremental revenue. Against an $80K engagement, that's a 12:1 revenue ROI.

**You don't need precise numbers. You need directionally credible numbers** — built from what the prospect told you, not from benchmarks you invented. Use their words: "You told us good looks like $600K per SDR. Today you're at $400K. Here's what closing that gap is worth."

#### How to Present It

Frame the investment as part of the model, not separate from it:

> "The cost of the current state is $X per year in wasted capacity. The system we're building recovers most of that and unlocks $Y in incremental pipeline. The investment to build it is $Z. That's a [ratio]:1 return in year one — before accounting for the pipeline that compounds in year two and beyond."

If the numbers are strong, let them do the selling. If they're not strong enough to justify the engagement on cost savings alone, the growth lever is usually what closes the gap.

### EB Path — Two Forks

**Path A: EB confirmed via champion (no formal EB meeting required)**

Use only if champion has explicitly stated the EB will sign off AND you've pressure-tested with confident answers to:
- "What have you told the EB about The Kiln and this problem?"
- "Why doesn't the EB need to be in the buying process?"
- "What would make the EB say no at the end?"
- "Has the EB been involved in a decision like this before — what did that look like?"

If the champion hesitates → default to Path B.

**Path B: Formal EB meeting required (default)**

Schedule before the SOW is finalized. Agenda: confirm priority to buy (I, M), intent to allocate funds (E), decision criteria (Dc), product differentiators with weighting and scoring. This is a gate — don't finalize the SOW without it.

### Our Best Questions

**Reinforce Market Forces**
- "Since we last spoke, has anything changed internally — new exec, new pressure, board conversation?"
- "How urgent is this relative to other priorities your team is running right now?"

**Lock in Future Vision**
- "When you described [X from discovery] — is that still the north star, or has it shifted?"
- "If we nailed this engagement, what would you be saying to your team 90 days from now?"
- "What's the one metric your EB would point to and say 'this worked'?"

**WHO — Champion**
- "Have you socialized this internally yet — what's the reaction been?"
- "Who else is going to have an opinion on this decision?"
- "If we were going to lose this, who would pump the brakes?"

**WHO — Economic Buyer**
- "Who controls the budget for something like this?"
- "What have you told them about this problem and about us?"

**HOW — Decision Criteria**
- "When you're evaluating agencies, what does the right fit look like?"
- "Is it methodology, outcome certainty, references from similar companies, pricing — what's weighted most?"
- "What would make you confident enough to move forward?"

**WHO ELSE — Competitors**
- "Are there other options you're considering — internally or externally?"
- "Who inside your org would advocate for a different approach?"

**Proof Points**
- "Do you want to talk to a reference in a similar situation before we finalize the SOW?"
- "I have a client in [similar space/size] who started where you are — want me to make an intro?"

**Scoping the Work**
- "If we could only fix one thing in the first 30 days, what would make the most internal difference?"
- "Which use case has an exec sponsor who's actively asking about it?"
- "What does your CRM/Salesforce state look like — clean foundation or do we need to start there?"
- "Do you have the bandwidth to be responsive to us daily?"

### Common Blockers
- **Champion hasn't sold this internally** — They like us but haven't created air cover. Deal dies when EB gets cold.
- **Future vision is vague** — "Better pipeline" is not a vision. Push for the specific metric and the specific day-in-the-life.
- **EB absent and champion can't answer pressure-test questions** — Schedule the EB meeting.
- **Decision criteria unknown** — You'll write the wrong SOW. Ask before you draft.
- **Competitor in play and unaddressed** — Surface it now.
- **Scope undefined** — Force prioritization before drafting.
- **Foundational problems** — Scope the foundation fix first.

## Stage 4: Statement of Work Finalized — 70%

The SOW is in front of the EB. The narrative arc is complete on paper. Now present it face-to-face, confirm the EB's alignment, and clear the path to signature.

MEDDPICC focus: **(E) Economic Buyer → (Dp) Decision Process → (P) Paper**

Narrative arc focus: **Walk the EB through all six elements in person. This is the business case presentation.**

### Exit Criteria
- [ ] **(E) Economic Buyer** has reviewed the SOW or been briefed; verbally aligned on priority, budget intent, and criteria
- [ ] **(Dp) Decision Process** mapped — who approves, in what order, by when
- [ ] SOW verbally approved by champion and EB
- [ ] Scope, deliverables, success criteria, and pricing explicitly agreed
- [ ] Budget approved by the financial decision-maker — not just the champion
- [ ] Start date confirmed
- [ ] **(P) Paper path** identified — procurement, NDA, IT onboarding, contractor approval scoped

### Presenting the Business Case to the EB

Walk through all six arc elements with the EB directly:

1. **Market Forces** — "Here's why your peers are moving on this now. The window to build this advantage is closing."
2. **Current Challenges** — "Here's what we heard. Here's the number that quantifies it."
3. **Future Vision** — "Here's what [champion] told us good looks like. Here's the target metric."
4. **Your Solution** — "Here's how we close that gap. Here's the track structure and the 30-day win."
5. **Proof Points** — "Here's a reference situation. Here's what they achieved."
6. **Next Steps** — "Here's the path. Here's the start date. Here's what we need from you."

Then let them drive the pricing conversation from the cost justification — not the price sheet.

### Our Best Questions
- "Does this SOW reflect what [champion] described as the priority? Anything you'd adjust?"
- "Walk me through how this gets approved on your end — who signs off and in what order?"
- "Is there anything that's going to be a hard no for legal or procurement?"
- "What's the process for getting a contractor approved in your system?"
- "What would need to be true for us to start on [date]?"

### Common Blockers
- **EB hasn't been briefed** — Champion assumed EB would rubber-stamp it. They didn't.
- **Decision process undefined** — Three approvals you didn't know about.
- **Enterprise compliance lag** — IT, contractor onboarding can push start dates 2-4 weeks.
- **Exec can block it late** — Know who the blocker is before you're surprised.
- **Champion can't close internally** — Give them the market forces + future vision language to carry in.

### Gate: Before Moving to Negotiation

- [ ] EB confirmed on buying steps — who approves, in what order, by when
- [ ] Detractors identified and neutralized — know who would pump the brakes and why, and address it before it surfaces in procurement

Detractors often surface after the SOW is circulated internally. Don't advance until you know who they are and have a plan. Go back to the champion if either of these is open.

## Stage 5: Negotiation + Procurement — 85%

Commercial alignment is done. Executing the **(P) Paper Process**.

### Exit Criteria
- [ ] Contract out for signature
- [ ] Legal/procurement process cleared or in final review
- [ ] Start date locked and confirmed
- [ ] Kickoff scheduled
- [ ] First deliverable or onboarding step defined

### Our Best Questions
- "Where does this sit right now — legal, finance, or someone else?"
- "Anything in the contract language you need us to adjust?"
- "What's the fastest this can move? We want to protect your start date."
- "What do you need from us to keep this moving?"

### Common Blockers
- **Budget approval lag** — Finance didn't know this was coming. A scoping failure, not a procurement problem.
- **Procurement process undefined** — Some companies have 6-week cycles. Surface at SOW stage.
- **Legal redlines** — Have a clean MSA ready to counter quickly.
- **IT/contractor onboarding** — Start early. System access and provisioning take longer than expected.

## Stage 6: Closed Won / Closed Lost

### Closed Won Signals (Patterns From Real Deals)
- Multiple stakeholders engaged across calls — not just one champion
- Champion was actively selling internally with our talking points and the narrative arc
- EB engaged early, understood the business case, aligned before SOW
- Decision criteria clear and SOW positioned against them
- Future vision captured in discovery, reflected verbatim in the SOW
- Budget confirmed early — not contingent on future approval
- Near-term wins tied to real internal goals
- Off-ramp discussed — internal hire, documentation, training plan

### Closed Lost Patterns (What Actually Killed Deals)

| Loss Reason | Framework Gap | Prevention |
|---|---|---|
| EB absent throughout | (E) never engaged | Require EB engagement at scoping |
| Budget timing mismatch | (E) not confirmed early | Surface budget at pre-qual |
| Timeline expectation gap — expected 1 month, we need 3 | (I) pain not deep enough to justify | State 3-month minimum at discovery |
| Team size ROI failure | (M) metrics didn't support cost | Screen team size at pre-qual |
| No urgency driver | Market Forces never established | Run market forces questions in discovery |
| Champion couldn't close internally | (C) weak champion, no EB alignment | Pressure-test champion at scoping |
| Lost to competitor | (Co) never surfaced | Ask at scoping, not at SOW |
| Future vision too vague | Arc element 3 incomplete | Push for specific metric and day-in-the-life |

- Set a follow-up sequence if timing was the only issue
- If it was a referral from 2X or Clay, close the loop with the referring team
