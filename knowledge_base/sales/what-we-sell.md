## The Outcomes We're Hired to Move

Every tool a company buys — including The Kiln — has to earn its place by moving one or more of five metrics. Cassie Young at Primary Ventures calls this the PRIME framework. Before you present the business case, identify which of these the prospect cares about most. That's the metric you build the SOW around.

*Source: Cassie Young, Primary Venture Partners*

---

### P — Productivity
**Revenue per employee. Time spent on Revenue Generating Activities.**

The most direct measure of GTM leverage. Are your people spending their time doing things that generate revenue, or doing things that could be automated? GTM engineering moves this by eliminating manual research, list building, routing, and prep — so every hour is a selling hour.

*In discovery: "How much of your reps' day is actually spent in front of buyers vs. building lists and doing research? What would that number need to be to hit your targets?"*

**Kiln impact:** Rep productivity, RGAs per day, pipeline per rep, time saved on admin.

---

### R — Retention
**Net Revenue Retention. Gross Revenue Retention. Does the system keep and grow customers?**

Retention is the most capital-efficient revenue there is — no new CAC, no new ramp, no new risk. GTM engineering improves retention by building expansion signal detection (who's ready to grow?), renewal risk signals (who's at risk of churning?), and the workflows that trigger action before it's too late.

*In discovery: "Do you have visibility into which customers are likely to expand or churn before it happens? What signals would tell you that?"*

**Kiln impact:** Expansion pipeline, renewal signal detection, customer health scoring, proactive outreach automation.

---

### I — Investment Efficiency
**CAC Payback. Magic Number. Board flash metrics.**

The metrics PE firms and boards track to evaluate whether GTM spend is working. GTM engineering improves investment efficiency by generating more pipeline per dollar spent — better targeting, higher conversion, shorter cycles. Every improvement in the funnel compresses CAC payback and moves the magic number.

*In discovery: "What's your CAC payback period today? What does the board track on GTM efficiency? Is there pressure to improve those numbers without increasing spend?"*

**Kiln impact:** CAC payback compression, magic number improvement, pipeline per dollar of GTM spend.

---

### M — Momentum
**Top-line growth. Pipeline velocity. Revenue acceleration.**

The board language metric. More pipeline, higher conversion rates, shorter sales cycles. This is what earns budget and attention — not cost savings, not efficiency gains, but the number going up. GTM engineering creates momentum by building the compounding infrastructure: better signals, better targeting, better personalization, faster action.

*In discovery: "What does the board point to as the primary growth metric? What would it take to move that number meaningfully in the next 12 months?"*

**Kiln impact:** Pipeline volume, conversion rates stage to stage, sales cycle compression, top-line ARR growth.

---

### E — Expense Reduction
**Tool consolidation. Headcount efficiency. Lower cost to serve.**

The floor of the business case, not the ceiling. Expense reduction alone doesn't get deals approved — but it makes the ROI case defensible to a CFO or COO who needs cost neutrality before they'll greenlight growth spend. Consolidating redundant data subscriptions, eliminating manual processes that require headcount, and building systems that scale without proportional cost — these all land here.

*In discovery: "Are there tools in your current stack you're paying for but not fully using? Are there manual processes today that require headcount you wouldn't need if the system ran automatically?"*

**Kiln impact:** Tool consolidation (ZoomInfo + Sales Intel + Hoover's → Clay), manual process elimination, headcount leverage.

---

**How to use PRIME in a deal:**

In discovery, identify which one or two of these the EB cares about most. That's the metric the business case is built around. The other metrics become supporting evidence.

A CRO cares about Momentum and Productivity. A CFO cares about Investment Efficiency and Expense Reduction. A PE operating partner cares about all five — but Investment Efficiency and Momentum are what show up in the portfolio review.

Never lead with Expense Reduction alone. It's the metric nobody gets promoted for maximizing. Lead with Momentum or Productivity, use Expense Reduction to make the cost case defensible.

**The warning PRIME also carries:**

Most AI projects sound interesting in a demo. Very few survive contact with PRIME. If the initiative doesn't improve one of those five things, it belongs in the toy box, not the operating plan.

The failure pattern Cassie called out directly: too many companies have 50 AI initiatives, unclear owners, fuzzy goals, and no real operating model. Everyone is experimenting. No one is accountable. That creates what she calls AI peanut butter — spread thin everywhere, meaningful nowhere.

The better model is centralized expertise with decentralized idea flow. Someone needs to make the work production-grade. Someone needs to decide which projects matter. Someone needs to translate the work into operating metrics. That's the role The Kiln plays. We're not asking every rep to become an AI power user. We build tools and push them into the surfaces reps already use — which lowers the change management tax and makes the system more durable.

The question was never "how do we get everyone to prompt better?" The question is: **how do we make the revenue system produce more with the same or better economics?** That's the right question. That's what PRIME keeps you anchored to.

## What We Build —  ~ Eight Main Workstreams

---

### 1. TAM Mapping

**What it is:** Defining a client's full addressable market, sourcing every account that fits, categorizing them by segment and fit score, and importing the complete picture into the CRM. Often the foundation everything else is built on — you can't prioritize a market you haven't mapped.

**How we build it:** The approach depends on how the market is structured. Common methods:
- **LinkedIn Sales Navigator** — for title and company filters
- **Clay lists + databases** — Apollo, ZoomInfo, Prospeo for broad coverage
- **Website scraping** — Firecrawl, Zenrows, or custom Python scrapers for markets that can't be queried through standard tools (niche industries, specific certifications, equipment types, local service providers)
- **Parallel web systems + Exa** — for more complex, context-based sourcing
- Categorization logic built on top to segment by industry, size, geography, fit tier, and intent

**The "before" state:** The org doesn't know the full universe of companies that should be in the CRM. Reps prospect into an undefined market using Sales Nav searches and gut feel. No one has a real answer to "what's our total market and how much of it have we penetrated?"

**Who buys it:** RevOps, VP Sales, CRO, CFO — this is often a business-level decision, not just a sales ops one. The stakes are higher than most buyers realize.

**Outcome:** A fully populated, scored CRM where every account in the TAM is categorized and tiered. Reps stop guessing. Leadership has a clear view of market coverage and whitespace.

**Why the stakes are higher than "reps working the right accounts":** An incomplete TAM doesn't just hurt pipeline — it creates organizational blind spots. When you don't know your full market, you're systematically missing revenue you don't know exists. And for growth-stage companies, a poorly defined TAM also impacts how valuable the company appears to investors — market sizing credibility matters at the board and fundraising level.

---

### 2. CRM Enrichment + Prioritization

**What it is:** Identifying which data fields matter for the client's GTM motion, sourcing them from the right providers, automating refresh cadences, and building scoring logic so the CRM stays accurate and actionable.

**The "before" state:** CRM has 50K accounts and 20K are usable. Fields are missing, stale, or wrong. Reps don't trust the data so they don't use it. Someone on the ops team is manually appending fields from ZoomInfo once a quarter.

**Who buys it:** RevOps, Marketing Ops — whoever owns CRM hygiene and is accountable for data quality.

**Outcome:** Clean, enriched, auto-refreshed CRM data. Scoring logic that tells reps which accounts to prioritize without manual triage. Marketing can prove attribution. Sales can trust the list.

---

### 3. Persona Classification + GTM Plays

**What it is:** Classifying contacts within target accounts by persona (role, seniority, buying influence) and mapping them to the right play — different personas get different sequences, angles, and triggers.

**The "before" state:** Everyone in the account gets the same generic outreach. The CMO and the SDR manager are receiving the same email. No one has mapped which persona cares about which problem or built different plays for each.

**Who buys it:** Demand Gen, Head of Sales Dev, VP Marketing — anyone running structured outbound with multiple buyer types.

**Outcome:** Each persona in a target account is in the right play with the right message. Response rates go up because the outreach is relevant. Reps aren't deciding who gets what — the system handles it.

---

### 4. Outbound Automation

**What it is:** Building the end-to-end outbound system — account sourcing, contact enrichment, signal detection, copy generation, and sequence enrollment. Two modes: fully automated and human-in-the-loop.

**The "before" state:** BDRs are spending 2–3 hours a day on research and list building before they send a single email. Personalization is copy-pasted from a template. Signal detection doesn't exist — outreach is calendar-driven, not trigger-driven. Sending infrastructure is ad hoc, deliverability is an afterthought, and inboxes aren't warmed.

**Who buys it:** Head of Sales Dev, VP Sales, CRO — anyone accountable for outbound pipeline.

**Outcome:** BDRs show up to a prioritized queue with research done, copy drafted, and signals surfaced. They spend their day executing conversations, not building lists. Pipeline from outbound becomes predictable and system-driven instead of rep-dependent.

**Fully automated outbound** — for high-volume, signal-triggered plays where speed matters more than rep review. Tools: SmartLead, Instantly, Heyreach (LinkedIn). We handle inbox setup, domain configuration, sending infrastructure, and deliverability optimization — warmup schedules, sending limits, bounce management, spam signal monitoring. The system sources the account, enriches the contact, generates the copy, and sends without rep involvement.

**Human-in-the-loop outbound** — for higher-touch accounts, enterprise targets, or any play where rep judgment adds value before the message goes out. Tools: Salesloft, Outreach, Gong Engage. We build pre-loaded, signal-triggered cadences — the research is done, the copy is drafted, signals are surfaced — and push them into rep-ready review queues inside the tools reps already use. One click to approve and send. No context switching, no manual research, no starting from scratch. The rep adds the judgment layer; the system handles everything upstream.

---

### 5. Inbound Enrichment + Routing

**What it is:** Enriching inbound leads in real-time at the point of form fill, scoring them, and routing to the right rep or sequence based on fit, intent, and segment — automatically.

**The "before" state:** Inbound leads hit a CRM and someone manually reviews them, decides if they're qualified, and assigns them. Speed-to-lead is measured in hours or days. High-fit accounts fall through because the routing logic doesn't exist.

**Who buys it:** RevOps, Marketing Ops, VP Marketing — whoever owns the handoff between marketing and sales.

**Outcome:** Every inbound lead is enriched, scored, and routed within minutes. High-fit accounts get immediate rep assignment or automated outreach. Marketing can prove pipeline influence. Speed-to-lead drops from hours to seconds.

---

### 6. Rep Workflows + Daily Prep

**What it is:** Automating the research and prep work a rep does before every call or account touch — account summaries, recent news, trigger events, contact history, suggested talking points — delivered in a format the rep can act on immediately.

**The "before" state:** Reps spend 30-60 minutes before each call on LinkedIn, Google, and the CRM piecing together context. Or they don't prep at all. Either way, discovery calls start cold.

**Who buys it:** VP Sales, CRO, RevOps — anyone trying to improve rep efficiency and call quality.

**Outcome:** Reps show up to every call with a brief that's already built. More time selling, less time researching. Call quality improves because the rep has context before the first question.

---

### 7. Event + Partner Outreach

**What it is:** Building the outreach system for a specific trigger — webinar, executive dinner, conference, partner co-sell motion. The system pulls the list, segments by deal stage or fit (late-stage get the dinner invite, early-stage get the webinar), matches the right case study or customer reference to each prospect, and generates personalized copy per segment.

**The "before" state:** Someone is manually pulling a list from the CRM, copy-pasting the same invite to 80 people, and guessing which case study to attach. High-value event slots go to the wrong accounts because there's no scoring logic.

**Who buys it:** Demand Gen, Field Marketing, VP Sales — anyone running event-based pipeline programs.

**Outcome:** Every event has a tiered, segmented invite list with personalized outreach ready to send. The right accounts get the right ask. Post-event follow-up is automated. Events convert to pipeline instead of just attendance.

---

### 8. Systems Integration + Reverse ETL

**What it is:** Connecting the tools that don't talk to each other. Getting data out of a warehouse, enrichment source, or third-party system and into the CRM or sales tool where reps can actually act on it.

**The "before" state:** Signals live in Snowflake, intent data sits in Demandbase, product usage data is in the data warehouse — and none of it surfaces in Salesforce or HubSpot where reps work. The data exists; it just never reaches the people who need it.

**Who buys it:** RevOps, Data/BI teams, CTO — anyone dealing with tool sprawl and data silos.

**Outcome:** A unified view in the CRM. Signals from every source — intent, product usage, enrichment, firmographic — surface where reps work. No more "we have the data but can't use it."

## The Common Thread

Every workstream follows the same pattern:

1. **A trigger or signal** — an inbound lead, a competitor signal, an event, an intent spike, a job change
2. **A list** — accounts and contacts that match the play
3. **Segmentation** — tiering by fit, stage, persona, or priority
4. **Context** — the research, brief, or social proof the rep needs
5. **Action** — outreach copy, sequence enrollment, routing, or rep notification

**The tasks we typically automate:**
- Building and pulling lists
- Segmenting and tiering accounts by fit, stage, or intent
- Researching accounts and contacts
- Aggregating multiple data sources into a single, complete picture of a company, segment, or list
- Prioritizing who to target and in what order
- Mapping and detecting signals
- Finding the warmest intro path into an account — mutual connections, shared backgrounds, existing relationships
- Identifying which current clients most closely resemble a prospect, so the rep leads with the most relevant story and social proof
- Adding contacts to the right sequences
- Drafting personalized outreach copy
- Routing inbound leads and pinging the right team member to act — at the right time, with the right context
- Building the brief a rep needs before a call
- Managing LinkedIn Ads audiences — uploading target company and contact lists, removing accounts already in pipeline or won, pruning disqualified accounts to minimize wasted ad spend
- Multithreading into accounts — identifying all the right contacts across a buying committee, figuring out what angle to take with each persona, and surfacing the warmest intro path into each one

We automate steps 1-4. The rep executes step 5.

**The North Star: Cost per Dollar Booked**

Every outcome we're hired to move should ultimately connect to this number. Saving reps time is nice — but time savings are unrealized gains that only get realized when they translate into more output per rep or fewer reps for the same output. Cost savings matter, but nobody enjoys cost-controlling their way to EBITDA growth. Revenue acceleration is the language boards speak: more pipeline, higher conversion rates, shorter sales cycles. That is what earns attention and budget. Frame every engagement outcome in those terms first.

**Rep Productivity**
More time in front of buyers, less time doing research, list building, and admin. Measured by revenue-generating activities per day, pipeline created per rep, and time-to-first-touch on new accounts.

**Speed to Lead**
Inbound leads enriched, scored, and routed in seconds instead of hours. The research shows the first responder wins the majority of the time — every minute of lag is lost conversion. Measured by time from form fill to first rep touch.

**Conversion Rates — Stage to Stage**
Better data, better context, and better targeting means more of the right conversations happen — and more of them advance. Measured by: MQL to SQL, SQL to opportunity, opportunity to close. A system that surfaces the right accounts with the right message at the right time moves all three.

**Pipeline Volume and Quality**
More qualified pipeline per SDR or AE, sourced from a defined and prioritized TAM rather than ad hoc prospecting. Measured by pipeline created per rep per quarter and average deal quality (size, fit, close rate).

**Market Coverage**
Percentage of the TAM that's been identified, enriched, tiered, and activated. Most companies are working a fraction of the accounts they should be — coverage gaps are invisible pipeline.

**Ad Spend Efficiency**
For companies running LinkedIn or paid ABM, dynamic audience management means spend is concentrated on accounts that are in-market and not yet won. Measured by CPL and pipeline influenced per dollar of ad spend.

**First-Mover Trust**
The signal isn't the advantage anymore — everyone has signals. The advantage is acting on them first, with a message precise enough that it doesn't feel like outreach. Being the first credible voice versus the tenth with similar messaging is the difference between a conversation and noise. Trust is built early or not at all. And once a buyer has a vendor they like, the bar to switch — even to something technically better — is higher than most assume. The system that compresses the distance between signal and action builds a trust advantage that compounds with every deal cycle.
